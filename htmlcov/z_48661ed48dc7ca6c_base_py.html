175 def get_s1s2(self, sampling: int = 200, distance: Quantity = 1 * meter) -> S1S2: 

176 r""" 

177 Compute the S1 and S2 scattering amplitude functions for a spherical scatterer. 

178 

179 The S1 and S2 parameters represent the scattering amplitudes for perpendicular and parallel polarizations of light, respectively. These parameters are fundamental in Mie theory, which describes the scattering of electromagnetic waves by spherical particles. 

180 

181 The formulas for \( S_1 \) and \( S_2 \) are: 

182 

183 .. math:: 

184 S_1 = \sum\limits_{n=1}^{n_{\text{max}}} \frac{2n+1}{n(n+1)} \left( a_n \pi_n + b_n \tau_n \right) \\ 

185 S_2 = \sum\limits_{n=1}^{n_{\text{max}}} \frac{2n+1}{n(n+1)} \left( a_n \tau_n + b_n \pi_n \right) 

186 

187 Where: 

188 

189 - \( a_n \) and \( b_n \): Mie coefficients, which depend on the size, shape, and refractive index of the scatterer. 

190 - \( \pi_n \) and \( \tau_n \): Angular functions related to the angular components of the incident and scattered fields. 

191 - \( n_{\text{max}} \): Maximum number of terms in the series, determined by the size parameter of the scatterer. 

192 

193 These scattering amplitude functions are essential for calculating properties such as scattering phase functions, efficiencies, and angular distribution of scattered light. 

194 

195 Parameters 

196 ---------- 

197 sampling : int 

198 The number of angular points used to sample the S1 and S2 functions. Higher sampling improves the resolution of the scattering pattern but increases computation time. 

199 distance : Quantity, optional 

200 The distance from the scatterer at which the S1 and S2 parameters are evaluated. This is typically set to 1 meter by default, but can be adjusted for specific setups. 

201 

202 Returns 

203 ------- 

204 S1S2 

205 An object containing the computed S1 and S2 parameters, representing the scattering amplitudes for the two polarization components. 

206 

207 Notes 

208 ----- 

209 - The S1 and S2 parameters are central to Mie scattering theory and are used to derive many important scattering properties, such as intensity distributions and polarization effects. 

210 - The `sampling` parameter controls how finely the angular distribution is resolved. A higher value of `sampling` provides more detailed scattering information, which can be critical for accurately modeling the far-field pattern. 

211 

212 Example 

213 ------- 

214 You can use this method to compute the scattering properties of spherical particles, particularly in experiments where the polarization and scattering pattern of the light are important. 

215 

216 Example usage: 

217 

218 >>> s1s2 = scatterer.get_s1s2(sampling=500) 

219 >>> print(s1s2.S1, s1s2.S2) 

220 

221 """ 

222 return S1S2(scatterer=self, sampling=sampling, distance=distance)

229

232 Q &= \big| E_x \big|^2 - \big| E_y \big|^2 \quad &\text{(linear polarization along x and y)} \\ 

233 U &= 2 \mathcal{Re} \big\{ E_x E_y^* \big\} \quad &\text{(linear polarization at +45° and -45°)} \\

235 

236 Where: 

237 

238 - \( I \): Total intensity of the scattered light. 

239 - \( Q \): The degree of linear polarization along the x and y axes. 

240 - \( U \): The degree of linear polarization at +45° and -45° to the axes.

242 

243 These parameters provide a powerful way to represent the full polarization state of the scattered light. 

244

247 sampling : int 

248 The number of angular points used to sample the Stokes parameters. A higher sampling value increases the resolution of the computed polarization pattern.

274 def get_far_field(self, sampling: int = 200, distance: Quantity = 1 * meter) -> FarField: 

275 r""" 

276 Compute the far-field scattering pattern for the scatterer. 

277 

278 The far fields describe the behavior of the scattered electromagnetic waves at a large distance from the scatterer, where the waves can be approximated as planar. 

279 The computed far fields represent the scattered electric field components in the directions parallel and perpendicular to the plane of incidence. 

280 

281 The far fields are computed as: 

282 

283 .. math:: 

284 \text{Fields} = E_{||}(\phi, \theta)^2, \, E_{\perp}(\phi, \theta)^2 

285 

286 These components represent the intensities of the scattered electric field in the parallel (\( E_{||} \)) and perpendicular (\( E_{\perp} \)) directions as functions of the spherical angles \( \phi \) (azimuthal) and \( \theta \) (polar). 

287 

288 The fields are expressed up to a constant phase factor: 

289 

290 .. math:: 

291 \exp{\left(-i k r \right)} 

292 

293 Where: 

294 

295 - \( k \): The wave number, related to the wavelength of the incident light. 

296 - \( r \): The distance from the scatterer (assumed to be large in the far-field approximation). 

297 

298 Parameters 

299 ---------- 

300 sampling : int 

301 The number of angular points used to sample the far-field pattern. A higher sampling value increases the angular resolution of the computed far-field intensities. 

302 distance : Quantity, optional 

303 The distance from the scatterer at which the far fields are evaluated. By default, this is set to 1 meter, but it can be adjusted to suit the specific experimental configuration. 

304 

305 Returns 

306 ------- 

307 FarField 

308 An object containing the computed far-field components (parallel and perpendicular intensities), which represent the angular distribution of the scattered light in the far field. 

309 

310 Notes 

311 ----- 

312 - The far-field approximation assumes that the distance from the scatterer is large enough that the curvature of the wavefronts can be neglected, and the fields can be treated as planar. 

313 - This method is useful for determining the angular distribution of the scattered light, which is critical in applications such as radar cross-section analysis, optical scattering experiments, and remote sensing. 

314 

315 Example 

316 ------- 

317 You can use this method to compute the far-field scattering pattern of a spherical scatterer, which is essential in understanding how the scatterer redirects light in the far field. 

318 

319 Example usage: 

320 

321 >>> far_field = scatterer.get_far_field(sampling=500) 

322 >>> print(far_field.E_phi, far_field.E_theta) 

323 

324 """ 

325 return FarField(scatterer=self, sampling=sampling, distance=distance)

328 r""" 

329 Compute the scattering phase function (SPF) for the scatterer. 

330 

331 The scattering phase function describes how the intensity of scattered light varies as a function of the scattering angles \( \theta \) (polar) and \( \phi \) (azimuthal). It is a key quantity in light scattering, as it characterizes the angular distribution of scattered light. 

332 

333 The scattering phase function is computed as: 

334 

335 .. math:: 

336 \text{SPF} = \sqrt{ E_{\parallel}(\phi, \theta)^2 + E_{\perp}(\phi, \theta)^2 } 

337 

338 Where: 

339 

340 - \( E_{\parallel} \): The parallel component of the scattered electric field. 

341 - \( E_{\perp} \): The perpendicular component of the scattered electric field. 

342 - \( \phi \) and \( \theta \): The azimuthal and polar angles, respectively, which describe the angular position of the scattered light. 

343 

344 The SPF combines the intensity contributions from both polarization components to describe the total scattered intensity in different directions. 

345 

346 Parameters 

347 ---------- 

348 sampling : int 

349 The number of angular points used to sample the scattering phase function. A higher sampling value increases the angular resolution of the computed SPF. 

350 distance : Quantity, optional 

351 The distance from the scatterer at which the scattering phase function is evaluated. By default, this is set to 1 meter, but it can be adjusted depending on the specific experimental configuration. 

352 

353 Returns 

354 ------- 

355 SPF 

356 An object containing the computed scattering phase function (SPF), representing the angular distribution of scattered light intensity. 

357 

358 Notes 

359 ----- 

360 - The scattering phase function is a critical quantity in the study of light scattering, as it provides insight into the directionality of the scattered light. It is commonly used in fields like atmospheric optics, biomedical imaging, and remote sensing. 

361 - The `sampling` parameter controls the angular resolution of the SPF. Higher sampling values yield more detailed scattering patterns, especially important when capturing small angular features. 

362 

363 Example 

364 ------- 

365 You can use this method to compute the scattering phase function of a spherical scatterer, helping to understand the angular distribution of light scattered by the particle. 

366 

367 Example usage:

375 def get_footprint(self, detector) -> Footprint: 

376 r""" 

377 Compute the footprint of the scattered light coupling with the detector. 

378 

379 The footprint represents the spatial distribution of the scattered light as it couples with the detector. 

380 This is important for understanding how the scattered light interacts with the detector’s field of view and is influenced by both the scatterer’s properties and the detector configuration. 

381 

382 The footprint is computed using an inverse Fourier transform: 

383 

384 .. math:: 

385 \big| \mathscr{F}^{-1} \big\{ \tilde{ \psi } (\xi, \nu),\ 

386 \tilde{ \phi}_{l,m}(\xi, \nu) \big\} 

387 (\delta_x, \delta_y) \big|^2 

388 

389 Where: 

390 

391 - \( \tilde{ \psi } (\xi, \nu) \): The Fourier transform of the scattered field. 

392 - \( \tilde{ \phi}_{l,m} (\xi, \nu) \): The Fourier transform of the detector’s capturing field. 

393 - \( \mathscr{F}^{-1} \): The inverse Fourier transform operator. 

394 - \( (\delta_x, \delta_y) \): Spatial coordinates describing the footprint in the detector plane. 

395 

396 The inverse Fourier transform yields the spatial pattern of the scattered light’s interaction with the detector, which is then squared to compute the intensity of the footprint. 

397 

398 Parameters 

399 ---------- 

400 detector : GenericDetector 

401 The detector object that defines the capturing field and geometry. This object provides information about the detector’s configuration, including its numerical aperture, position, and sensitivity. 

402 

403 Returns 

404 ------- 

405 Footprint 

406 An object containing the computed scatterer footprint, representing the spatial distribution of the scattered light on the detector plane. 

407 

408 Notes 

409 ----- 

410 - The footprint provides valuable information about the coupling efficiency between the scattered light and the detector. This is useful in designing experiments where precise control of light detection is important, such as in microscopy, remote sensing, or optical measurements. 

411 - The interaction between the scattered field and the detector is influenced by factors such as the size, shape, and refractive index of the scatterer, as well as the detector’s aperture and positioning. 

412 

413 Example 

414 ------- 

415 You can use this method to compute the footprint of the scattered light on a detector, helping to visualize how light from the scatterer is spatially distributed on the detector. 

416 

417 Example usage: 

418 

419 >>> footprint = scatterer.get_footprint(detector=my_detector) 

420 >>> print(footprint) 

421 

422 """ 

423 return Footprint(scatterer=self, detector=detector)
